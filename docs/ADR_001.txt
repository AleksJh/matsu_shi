Engineering Deterministic AI Agents: Why we decoupled Control and Data Planes in Matsu Shi

Building AI for industrial maintenance leaves zero room for the "non-deterministic" drift common in basic LLM wrappers. For the Matsu Shi project, our first Architectural Decision Record focused on moving away from monolithic frameworks toward a Hybrid Orchestration model.

1. Decoupling the Control Plane from the Data Plane
We separated the "reasoning" from the "retrieval" to ensure system stability:
- Control Plane (PydanticAI): We use PydanticAI to enforce strict Type Safety and structured outputs. By treating the LLM as a typed function, we ensure the agent's decisions are validated against our schema before they ever reach the user.
Data Plane (LlamaIndex): We offloaded the heavy lifting of PDF parsing, complex RAG pipelines, and metadata management to LlamaIndex. It excels at high-density technical data ingestion, which PydanticAI isn't built for.

2. From Stateless Chats to Stateful Workflows
One-shot RAG fails when diagnosing complex hydraulic systems. We implemented Stateful Agentic Workflows where:
The system maintains an Active Diagnostic State in PostgreSQL.
Complex queries trigger a Step-by-Step mode, generating a roadmap rather than a wall of text.
Persistence: A mechanic can pause a 10-step repair, and the agent resumes with the exact context of the last measured pressure or bolt torque, preventing context loss.

3. Reliability through Metadata & Evals
We don't trust "vibes." Every retrieval is gated by a retrieval_score threshold. If the hybrid search (Dense + Sparse) returns a low-confidence score, the system triggers an Escalation Path to a larger model (Gemini 3 Pro) or asks for human clarification instead of hallucinating a fix.
